THE CELLIST OF SARAJEVO — REVIEW, ANALYSIS, AND ANNOTATIONS

FILE #97

Academic Reconstruction Record — Prepared for External Review.

Ministry Classification: Public Access — Educational Use.

Author Information

Collected for context. Some information was incomplete.

  • Steven Galloway is a Canadian novelist who lives in Vancouver and has taught Creative Writing at the University of British Columbia.
  • The Cellist of Sarajevo was published in 2008 and is one of his best-known novels.
  • The novel is inspired by the real Siege of Sarajevo during the Bosnian War.
  • The cellist character is based on the real musician Vedran Smailović.
  • Many of Galloway's works explore psychological survival, memory, and the human cost of conflict.

Steven Galloway is best known for The Cellist of Sarajevo, a novel inspired by the real Siege of Sarajevo and the actions of cellist Vedran Smailović, who famously performed in the city's ruined streets after civilians were killed in a mortar attack.

What interests me most about Galloway's writing is that he rarely focuses on war itself. Instead, he focuses on the people trapped inside it. The novel is far more concerned with survival, memory, fear, and identity than with military strategy or political conflict.

As a result, the characters never feel larger than life. They feel exhausted. They feel frightened. Most importantly, they feel real. Reading the novel often feels less like watching a war story and more like quietly observing people trying to remain human while the world around them falls apart.

DISCLAIMER ABOUT THE AUTHOR

Steven Galloway has faced allegations involving sexual assault, physical assault, and harassment. I was unaware of these allegations when I first read the novel, and I have not researched them extensively enough to form a fully informed opinion regarding every claim.

I do not support or condone abuse, harassment, or violence in any form. I am including this information for transparency and because some readers may feel that an author's actions influence how their work should be viewed.

At the same time, I believe it is possible to discuss and analyze a novel as a separate work of fiction. Readers should research the subject themselves and arrive at their own conclusions.

Brief Summary

The Cellist of Sarajevo follows several civilians struggling to survive during the Siege of Sarajevo.

After twenty-two people are killed while waiting in line for bread, a cellist makes the decision to perform at the site of the attack for twenty-two consecutive days in memory of the victims.

From that simple premise, the novel explores survival, morality, fear, memory, and what it means to remain human during prolonged violence.

What makes the story unique is that it is not particularly concerned with plot. Instead, it focuses on atmosphere and psychology. Much of the tension comes from ordinary tasks becoming life-threatening. Carrying water is dangerous. Crossing a street is dangerous. Existing becomes dangerous.

To me, that transformation of everyday life is one of the novel's most powerful ideas. War does not only destroy buildings. It destroys normality itself.

Purpose of the Novel

I believe the novel exists, at least in part, to remind readers that war is often viewed from a distance. We learn about battles, borders, and political decisions, but rarely stop to consider the ordinary people forced to live through them.

Rather than focusing on soldiers or military victories, the novel centers on civilians attempting to survive one day at a time. People still need food, water, sleep, and companionship. The siege transforms these ordinary needs into dangerous challenges.

What I find particularly effective is Galloway's focus on quiet moments. The novel never romanticizes conflict. Instead, it presents war as repetitive, exhausting, and deeply dehumanizing.

At the same time, it argues that beauty still matters. Memory still matters. Compassion still matters.

The Cellist's music does not stop the violence surrounding him, but that is not really the point. His performances become an act of resistance. They are a refusal to let violence define everything. In a world determined to reduce people to victims, enemies, or statistics, the music reminds them that they are still human beings.

Why This Record Was Chosen

I originally chose this novel because I am a musician myself. I play several instruments, though flute, piano, and guitar are the ones I spend the most time with. The idea of a man responding to violence not with weapons, but with music, immediately fascinated me.

There is something deeply human about creating beauty in a place designed to destroy it. Music may not physically change the world, but it can change how people experience and understand it.

I had also previously read novels centered around war, philosophy, and psychological survival, so many of the themes felt familiar. What surprised me was how intimate this novel felt compared to other war stories.

The most memorable moments are often the smallest ones: carrying water, hearing footsteps, or remembering who you were before fear became part of everyday life.

What interested me most was the philosophical question beneath the story. If survival forces a person to gradually sacrifice parts of themselves, are they still the same person afterward?

The novel constantly returns to questions of identity, memory, and humanity. It portrays war not only as physical destruction, but also as a force capable of reshaping people internally until they barely recognize themselves.

That is ultimately why the novel stayed with me. Some readers may find parts repetitive, but I think the repetition serves a purpose. War is repetitive. Fear is repetitive. Survival is repetitive.

People wake up every day and continue because they have no alternative. Yet despite that exhaustion, the novel continues to argue that beauty, compassion, and dignity still matter. I think that message is what makes the story so memorable.

Final Evaluation

Personal Rating:
★★★★☆ (4/5)

I would highly recommend this novel to readers interested in psychological fiction, philosophy, war literature, and human behavior during times of crisis.

Rather than focusing on action, the novel explores fear, identity, morality, memory, and emotional survival in a way that feels deeply human.

At times, the novel can be emotionally exhausting, but I believe that is intentional. It forces readers to sit with discomfort rather than escape from it.

The quiet moments often carry more weight than the violent ones because they remind us that the people suffering are not symbols or statistics. They are ordinary individuals trying to preserve fragments of themselves.

I would not recommend the novel to very young readers due to its mature themes, discussions of violence, and psychological intensity. While it is never graphic simply for shock value, it requires a certain level of emotional maturity to fully appreciate its ideas.

Ultimately, I think the novel succeeds because it refuses to let humanity disappear completely.

It does not offer hope in the traditional sense. Instead, it suggests that even in circumstances designed to destroy people, there are still moments of beauty, compassion, and connection worth protecting.
