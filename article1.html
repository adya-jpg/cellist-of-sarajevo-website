Overall Themes

Humanity During War

Every character is trying not to lose themselves completely, holding on to their thin strands of humanity and sanity through memory. But war pushes people into a sort of mindset where humanity is a privilege. Or at least that is what many people believe.

Art as Resistance

The cello symbolizes emotional resistance, the idea that no matter what war takes from you. It can never take away your ability to spread joy and beauty. It can never take the human urge to live.

Memory + Identity

The novel repeatedly asks: if trauma forces someone to replace every emotional part of themselves, are they still the same person afterward? Each character answers this question differently.

Character Records

The Cellist.

Based on a real person.

After twenty-two civilians are killed while waiting in line for bread, he plays the cello at the site of the attack for twenty-two consecutive days. Despite the danger. Despite the war. Despite the ugliness surrounding him.

He chooses to create beauty. He plays not to stop the war, but to remind the city that beauty still exists within it.

At times, he feels less like a man and more like a final act of defiance. His music gathers the remaining fragments of humanity and sanity scattered throughout Sarajevo and weaves them into something beautiful and tragic: a tapestry of love, life, grief, and hope.

By the twenty-second day, the city no longer sees a man with a cello. It sees proof that humanity has not disappeared completely.

He is the closest thing the novel has to a phoenix: the first sign of Sarajevo rising from its own ashes.

Arrow

A female sniper assigned to protect the Cellist.

She considers herself a weapon rather than a person. A soldier separated from her former identity. She survives by becoming someone new entirely.

War has forced her through a metamorphosis fueled by anger, grief, and necessity.

She has become everything she believes she must be: a weapon forged from hatred, yet still haunted by the person she once was.

Kenan

A father attempting to collect water for his family.

He considers himself a coward for fearing death, even though that fear is entirely human. He desperately needs help, yet refuses to ask for it.

Like many parents, he believes his own life matters less than the lives of those he loves.

He chooses to help his neighbour, even when he cannot fully explain why. Deep down, however, he understands. It is his way of bringing light back into the world.

By helping others in small ways, he behaves as though Sarajevo is still a normal city. In doing so, he helps preserve a piece of what the city once was.

Dragan

An older man who works at a bakery.

He is lonely, like almost everyone in the novel. The difference is that he chooses isolation.

Perhaps he believes that if he avoids seeing what war has done to the people around him, he can preserve the memory of the Sarajevo he once loved.

He clings to memories because they are often the only beautiful things left.

Recovered Annotation Records.

The Fall of Sarajevo.

Notes:

The men on the hills surround Sarajevo and fire upon civilians. They shoot at people carrying bread and water as though these necessities were weapons. What strikes me most is not the violence itself, but the reasoning behind it. They claim it is because Sarajevo hates Sarajevo, as though the city is somehow responsible for its own suffering.

Yet they fail to recognize the role they play in creating that hatred. The resentment that consumes the city did not simply appear on its own. It was nurtured by fear, violence, and loss. In many ways, the men on the hills have caused Sarajevo to fall out of love with itself. The city is no longer able to see itself as it once did because it has spent too long looking down the barrel of a gun.

Philosophical Significance.

  • The novel suggests that hatred is often something that is created rather than something people are born with.
  • The men on the hills become difficult to hate when viewed as individual people instead of faceless soldiers.
  • As Zipf's Principle states, "Each individual will adopt a course of action that will involve the expenditure of the least possible amount of effort."
  • It is easier to hate an idea, a group, or an enemy than it is to hate a person.
  • Both sides come to view each other as entities rather than collections of individuals, making violence easier to justify.

Keeper's Annotation

Have you ever read "On the Sidewalk Bleeding" by Evan Hunter?

The story follows a boy named Andy who is stabbed because he belongs to a gang called The Royals. He is not attacked because he is Andy. He is attacked because he is a Royal.

I think that idea appears throughout this novel as well. It is surprisingly easy to hate a group. A group has no face. It has no voice. It does not laugh, cry, or tell stories. But a person does.

Once you recognize someone's humanity, hatred becomes more complicated. Pulling the trigger becomes harder. Violence becomes harder. And if you do go through with it, I do not think you leave unchanged.

Society may call you a monster afterward, but very few people stop to ask how someone became capable of doing monstrous things. Maybe that question does not excuse the action, but it matters.

The memory remains either way. It lingers like blood that never fully washes from your hands. Your hands are stained. Your soul is stained.

Haunted by Lady Death.

Notes

Arrow fascinates me because she is a contradiction made flesh. Like nearly every character in this novel, she is haunted by death. The difference is that she has learned to walk beside it.

She is tasked with protecting the Cellist and everything he represents, yet she does so through violence. She preserves beauty through the very thing capable of destroying it. In a strange way, that contradiction defines her entire existence.

Arrow protects everything she loves with the thing she hates most. She avoids thinking too deeply about her actions because she understands what reflection might do to her resolve. The girl she once was would object. The person she has become knows that hesitation could mean failure.

What makes her compelling is not her ability to kill, but the fact that part of her never stops questioning whether she should.

Philosophical Significance

  • Arrow views herself as a weapon long before she sees herself as a person.
  • She represents the contradiction between destruction and protection.
  • Her courage comes not from fearlessness, but from continuing despite the emotional weight she carries.

Keeper's Annotation

Have you ever read "The Diamond Eye" by Kate Quinn?

One of the characters, Mila, reminds me of Arrow. Both women are shaped by violence, yet neither is fully defined by it.

They continue carrying pieces of who they used to be while adapting to who they need to become. To me, that is what makes both characters memorable. They never completely lose their humanity, even when the world gives them every reason to.

The Rise of a New Sarajevo.

Notes

By the end of the novel, every major character changes in some way. What I find interesting is that none of them overcome fear completely. Instead, they learn to live alongside it.

Fear dominates much of the story. It influences decisions, relationships, and daily routines. Yet the characters eventually reach a point where they choose not to let fear decide everything for them.

That choice is what creates the possibility of a new Sarajevo. Not a perfect Sarajevo, and not the Sarajevo that existed before the war, but a city capable of continuing forward despite everything that has happened to it.

Character Transformation

  • Arrow reconnects with the person she once was. Rather than spending her final moments acting solely as a weapon, she allows herself to become a fractured mixture of both Alisa and Arrow.
  • Kenan realizes that the small things he does every day are not meaningless. Carrying water, helping others, and continuing to live all become acts of resistance that help preserve the spirit of Sarajevo.
  • Dragan comes to understand that hope is impossible if fear controls every aspect of his life. When he chooses to truly live again, he also creates the possibility for others to do the same.

Keeper's Annotation

It's a beautiful book.

I honestly struggle to describe it properly because nearly everything in the novel feels intentional. Every repeated action. Every quiet moment. Every ordinary task that suddenly becomes terrifying.

Some of my colleagues argue that the novel is too repetitive. I understand why they feel that way.

But I think that repetition is the point.

War is repetitive. Fear is repetitive. Survival is repetitive.

People wake up each day and repeat the same exhausting routines, hoping they will survive long enough to see tomorrow. Days blur together. Nights blur together. Smoke replaces the sky. Fear becomes normal.

Eventually, a person stops living and starts existing.

That realization is devastating in a way that is difficult to explain. You are technically alive, but every part of life that once made you feel human begins to disappear.

That is why I believe this novel succeeds.

It is not simply a story about war. It is an attempt to communicate what war feels like for ordinary civilians trapped inside it. The confusion. The exhaustion. The grief. The determination to keep moving forward even when there seems to be no reason left to do so.

More than anything else, it is a reminder that the people caught in history are still people.

Recovered Questions

  • Does beauty still matter if it cannot truly stop suffering?
  • Is emotional numbness survival or self-destruction?
  • At what point does adaptation become transformation?
  • Can morality survive prolonged violence?
  • What actually makes someone human?
